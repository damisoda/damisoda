Grace Lee Boggs spent seventy years in Detroit answering that through what she called "(r)evolution" — not the seizure of power but the transformation of values, place, and relationships. She married James Boggs, embedded herself in Black radical politics, refused both the position of model minority and that of neutral observer. By the time Grace Lee filmed her, she had moved past Marxist-Leninist orthodoxy toward something more philosophical: dialectical thinking about how human beings grow, urban agriculture as politics, the idea that revolution happens "at the speed of community."
Sa-I-Gu shows what the absence of that framework looks like. Dai Sil Kim-Gibson and Christine Choy let Korean immigrant women — mothers, shopkeepers — speak in the raw aftermath of the uprising. Many had no political vocabulary for what had just happened to them. They came to America with a script about hard work and meritocracy, and the burning of their stores didn't fit. On camera, some of them begin constructing a new political consciousness in real time — Jung Hui Lee famously realizes she is a "colored" person in America, that her interests might lie with Black and Brown people rather than against them. That awakening is exactly what Boggs had been theorizing for decades.<h1 align="center">Hi, I'm Daeheum 👋</h1>
